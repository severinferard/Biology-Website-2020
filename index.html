  1. First experiments

    AD 157

    In AD 157, the Greek physician Galen observed the still-beating hearts in dying gladiators' bodies. A few years later after moving to Rome, he conducted some experiments on pigs and monkeys to analyse their pulmonary circulation system. He suggested that arterial blood came from the heart and venous blood from the liver, and that the liver was responsible for sending blood to the body.

    Portrait of Galen. Georg Paul Busch. 18th century. CC0 1.0

  2. Heart related discoveries

    1500s

    Realdo Colombo (1515-1559), an Italian surgeon, researched pulmonary circulation. He found that the valves of the heart allows blood to flow unidirectionally: from the right ventricle to the lungs and back to the left ventricle, and then to the aorta.

    Portrait of R. Colombo. CC0 1.0

  3. The circuit completed

    1600s

    In the late 1500s and early 1600s, the British physician William Harvey published some measurements about the heart and its functions. He described the estimated weight of the heart and the amount of blood pumped every hour. Then, he understood that blood flow was organised between two loops: the pulmonary circulation between the heart and the lungs, and the systemic circulation between the heart and the rest of the body. Nowadays, Harvey is known as the man who discovered the functioning of blood circulation.

    Portrait of W. Harvey. Unknown author. CC BY-SA 4.0
