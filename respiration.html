Integration of the air dioxygen to the circulatory system
Since the cells of an organ need O2 to produce their energy through cellular respiration, the blood system transports O2 to deliver them. In exchange, blood brings CO2, a product of respiration, back to the heart and lungs in order to evacuate it.
Basic principles of gases

A gas is a state of matter in which particles are spread out. In gas, each particle is in movement and has a kinetic energy. Because of this movement, particles can meet and collide with each others. The force driven by this collide is called pressure.

Diffusion


The diffusion is a displacement of molecules that tend to homogenize the composition of the medium. A gas always diffuses from the region with the highest partial pressure to the region with the lowest pressure because of the concentration gradient. 

The concentration gradient is the difference between the concentration of molecules on either side of a thin membrane. 

Diffusion occurs in solutions too. Indeed, like in gas, molecules circulate from the most concentrated regions to the less ones.

Thus, whether the gas is in gaseous form or solubilized in a solvent, its movements will be dictated by diffusion.

Osmosis


It is a type of diffusion in which water goes from one side of the membrane to the other one to equilibrate the concentration. It will dilute the most concentrated part and concentrate the other one.

Different specialized breathing surfaces


Membrane transport of O2 and CO2 molecules is carried out entirely by simple diffusion. The larger the diffusion surface and the shorter the diffusion distance, the faster the gas exchange. This is why breathing surfaces are generally thin and extensive. Gas exchange conditions vary greatly, depending on whether the source of oxygen, called the respiratory environment, is air or water.

This breathing surface consists of the airways that connect the atmospheric air to the alveolar air in the lungs.



The inhalation begins with the contraction of the diaphragm and rib muscles which will lead to increasing the volume of lung. Thanks to that air will move into the respiratory tract through the nasal fossae, pharynx, larynx, trachea and bronchial tubes until alveoli. Thanks to diffusion, gas will exchange between alveoli and blood.

 

The exhalation is the opposite mechanism.  It begins with the relaxation of diaphragm and rib muscles. So the volume of lungs decreases. Gas in blood will move into the alveoli and leave our body by nose or mouth.

 

 






These pathways have a conductive role, and also serve to warm, moisturize and remove dust from the air. To deal with this last function, secretory cells produce mucus which trap the various particles. There are also hairs on cells that move these particles to the pharynx.

 

Lungs are divided into lobes (for humans: 3 on the right and 2 on the left). In the lobe of a lung, we find a network of bronchus which are finished by the bronchioles, at the end of which, are the alveolar sacs. This structure is known as a bronchial tree.

 

The wall of the alveoli is thin and highly vascularized. Thanks to that, diffusion can occur. Indeed, the partial pressure of O2 is higher in the air that we inhale than in the artery. As a result, O2 moves from the air to the artery.

Conversely, CO2 moved toward the opposite direction of O2. The partial pressure of CO2 is higher in the veins than in the air that we inhaled. So CO2 moves from the veins to the air.

This mechanism is called osmotic pressure



The cells in the wall are pneumocytes. There are two groups of pneumocytes: the primary ones which allow gas exchanges and the secondary ones which secrete a liquid (the surfactant), a mixture of phospholipids (90%) and proteins (10%). The latter will form a film on the surface of the alveoli. This liquid prevents the cells from folding back on themselves and facilitates gas exchanges.

The gas diffuses through the wall whose total surface area is very large (humans have 300 millions alveoli per lung), around 70 m2 per lung.

Amphibians


Amphibians are quite special because they change their breathing system during their life. Larva have gills, but adults use lungs. Gills are generally not useful to terrestrial animals. This is because a large moistened membrane surface exposed to the air would lose too much water through spraying. In addition, the gills would collapse because the fine filaments of their structure would no longer float in the water and would form a compact mass. In most land animals, therefore, the breathing surfaces are inside the body and communicate with the outside world through the gills.

The oxygen supply is due to inhalation of air. But most amphibians are able to exchange gases in water or air thanks to their skin if their skin is wet enough.

An Amphibian, like a frog, ventilates its lungs through a positive pressure breathing mechanism, which inflates the lungs by forcing air into them thanks to the expansion of the buccal cavity.

 

Remember, the ideal gas law states that if the volume of a mixture of gases increases, the pressure decreases. Consequently the pressure inside the buccal cavity will decrease, resulting in a pressure gradient between the buccal cavity and the lungs, and exterior environment. This gradient causes air to be drawn from the outside to the inside of the buccal cavity and from the lungs to the buccal cavity.

 

The elastic relaxation of the lungs and the compression by the muscles of the body force air out of the lungs during exhalation.

Insects


Insects have spiracles on their exoskeleton to allow air to enter the trachea. In insects, the tracheal tubes mainly deliver oxygen directly into the insect tissues.  The spiracles can be opened and closed effectively to reduce water loss. This is done by contracting the muscles surrounding the spiracle. To open, the muscle relaxes. 

These muscles are controlled by the central nervous system but can also react to localized chemical stimuli. Many aquatic insects have similar methods of closure to prevent water from entering the trachea. Spiracles can also be surrounded by hair to minimize air movement around the opening, thus minimizing water loss.

 

Spiracles are located laterally along the thorax and abdomen of most insects (usually one pair of spiracles per body segment). Air flow is regulated by small muscles that operate one or two flap valves in each spiracle that contract to close the spiracle, or relax to open it.

Birds


The active phase of breathing in birds is exhalation, requiring a contraction of their respiratory muscles. Relaxation of these muscles produces inhalation.

When a bird breathes, the air passes through the gas exchange surface in only one direction. The new air entering does not mix with the old air that has participated in the gas exchange.

Unlike mammals, birds have aerian bags. Most birds have 9 bags. During inhalation, all air bags fill, and during exhalation, all air bags empty.





Instead of having alveoli, which are dead ends, birds lungs have thin ducts called parabronchus.

Birds lungs are proportionally smaller than mammalian lungs compared to their body size. They are also rigid, meaning that their volume does not change during respiration.

Fishes


The gills are vascularized tissues that form a respiratory exchange surface. They are the main specialized gas exchange surface in aquatic animals.




    Composition of gills

The gills of vertebrate typically develop in the walls of the pharynx, along a series of gill slits opening to the outside. 

A fish has four gills under each operculum. A gill consists of a bone (gill arch) on which two gill blades made of fine lamella are inserted (about 200 per blade). Each lamella is very richly irrigated and the blood is separated from the water by only a few micrometres or even less than one micrometre.




    How gills work

Water enters through the mouth and exits through the gills, with sufficient water flow created by the animal's movement and/or by pumping movements of the mouth or gills. In the process, the blood captured oxygen dissolved in the water and released carbon dioxide previously dissolved in the blood by osmotic diffusion.
